\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Our detection pipeline was designed with two primary goals in mind: maximizing the forensic visibility of digital tampering and ensuring the classification architecture remained lightweight enough for practical deployment. Achieving these goals required a careful, multi-step process, combining image processing techniques with an optimized deep learning framework.

\subsection{Data Curation and Preprocessing}

To train and validate our model, we relied on a carefully curated subset of the FaceForensics++ \cite{faceforensics} dataset. We extracted a total of 10,000 static frames, ensuring a perfectly balanced distribution of 5,000 pristine (authentic) images and 5,000 manipulated (deepfake) images. The manipulated subset included a variety of synthesis techniques, ensuring our model learned generalized compression artifacts rather than overfitting to the quirks of a single generator.

Before any image was fed into the neural network, we passed it through our custom Error Level Analysis (ELA) pipeline. ELA is the cornerstone of our methodology. Let an input image be denoted as $I$. We defined the re-compression operation $\mathcal{C}_{q}$, which compresses an image using the JPEG algorithm at a specific quality factor $q$. For our experiments, we fixed $q = 90$, as empirical testing showed this value yielded the highest contrast for compression disparities. The intermediate re-compressed image $I'$ is generated by:
\begin{equation}
    I' = \mathcal{C}_{90}(I)
\end{equation}

The raw ELA difference matrix, $D$, is then calculated by taking the absolute pixel-wise difference between the original image and its re-compressed counterpart:
\begin{equation}
    D = |I - I'|
\end{equation}

Because the raw difference values in $D$ are often extremely small and difficult for a neural network to interpret as primary features, we applied a brightness scaling multiplier. We scaled the pixel intensities of $D$ to span the full $0-255$ 8-bit color range, maximizing the visual contrast. This operation effectively strips away the natural semantic content of the original image, leaving behind a stark heatmap that visually represents the latent compression grid. In these heatmaps, heavily manipulated regions---such as a synthesized face---appear drastically brighter and noisier than the surrounding authentic background.

\subsection{Network Architecture Selection}

With our ELA heatmaps generated, we needed a classification backbone. We ultimately selected the EfficientNet-B0 architecture \cite{efficientnet}. Our choice was driven by the need for parameter efficiency. While models like ResNet-50 or VGG-16 are powerful, they are exceedingly heavy. EfficientNet uses a compound scaling method to carefully balance network depth, width, and resolution, allowing it to achieve state-of-the-art feature extraction while remaining computationally inexpensive. 

We adapted the standard EfficientNet-B0 architecture for our specific binary classification task. First, we stripped away the original 1000-class ImageNet classification head. We replaced it with a custom top-level module consisting of a global average pooling layer, followed by a dense layer featuring a single linear node. Because we formulated the deepfake detection problem as a binary task (Real vs. Fake), this single node output, coupled with a Binary Cross-Entropy with Logits loss function, provided the most direct mathematical representation of our objective.

\subsection{The Challenge of Transfer Learning on ELA}

Perhaps the most significant challenge we faced during our methodology design was navigating the domain shift between natural imagery and ELA heatmaps. Like most modern computer vision practitioners, we initially attempted a standard transfer learning approach: we loaded the EfficientNet-B0 base pre-trained on ImageNet, froze the convolutional layers to preserve their learned filters, and only trained our new classification head.

This approach failed spectacularly. Because the pre-trained EfficientNet base was optimized on ImageNet---a dataset composed of natural photos containing smooth gradients, clear edges, and recognizable shapes---its lower-level convolutional filters were completely unequipped to understand our input data. ELA heatmaps do not look like natural images; they resemble abstract, high-frequency, grid-like noise. The frozen filters were simply passing garbage activations up to our classification head.

To solve this, we implemented full network fine-tuning combined with a strategy of differential learning rates. Instead of freezing the base network, we completely unfroze it. However, we recognized that the base network still held valuable, generalized edge-detection capabilities that we didn't want to destroy. Therefore, we applied a very small learning rate ($\alpha_{base} = 5 \times 10^{-5}$) to the pre-trained feature extractor, allowing its filters to slowly adapt to the unique textures of compression noise without experiencing catastrophic forgetting. Simultaneously, we applied a much larger, standard learning rate ($\alpha_{head} = 1 \times 10^{-3}$) to our newly initialized classification head, allowing it to rapidly converge on the binary task. This nuanced approach to transfer learning proved to be the critical unlock for our model's performance.
